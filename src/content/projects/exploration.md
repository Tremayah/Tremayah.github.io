Sandwich Lamp refers to a series of lamps I made for a batch production module in university. 4 lamps were made in total: 3 square, and one long lamp created from offcuts. The lamp explores how light and layered acrylic interact, as well as the construction of simple geometrical forms.

## Initial ideas

In a previous project, a test cube was built from sheets of acrylic bonded with a plastic-weld chemical, sanded down, then sandblasted. The cube interacted with light in a fascinating way, making me curious of the possibilities of layered acrylic.

The cube was modelled in Rhino, before being sliced into layers. Originally 100mm across, it grew to 200mm to better suit a traditional bulb — but doubling the size doubled the number of 5mm layers from sixteen to thirty-two. Across ten lamps that meant more than three hundred panels of acrylic. At roughly £12 for a 600 × 600 × 5mm sheet, a single 200mm lamp worked out at nearly £43 in material alone — far too high for one of ten units. Downsizing fixed it: at 120mm the same lamp cost under £10, and the design was rescaled accordingly.

## Research

A recurring weakness in my past lighting projects has been the finer technical detail, so this time the lighting came first. I settled on a prewired LED module — initially reluctant to solder, and put off by its cool 6500K colour, but eventually committing to soldering for the cleaner result. For the switch I chose a chunky chrome toggle over a button, specifically because I wanted it to stick out from the body of the lamp and feel satisfying to flick.

Before committing to a single form, I brainstormed eight different shapes for a layered-acrylic light. Design #7 stood out as both achievable and useful — the space in its opaque side could hold a prewired light or hide the wiring.

## Prototyping

The first shape I built was a triangular prism — colloquially, the “Toblerone Lamp.” It was glued up from many laser-ply layers cut with holes for the wire, the light, and alignment dowels. Plans for a spray-painted, typographic base were dropped once it became clear that the uneven surface of the layered ply would need far too much preparation.

Combining many acrylic layers accurately proved difficult. A simple alignment jig didn't help — even tiny tolerances added up into a warped final piece. The answer was to switch to a square form with fewer layers, which also cut material cost.

## The sketch model

The square scale model began in cardboard, just to judge proportion, before being rebuilt in real materials: knurled thumb bolts, laser-cut acrylic squares with room for the cables and switch, and brass and rubber nuts originally intended for a motorcycle. The first version had a definite homemade charm, but with no opaque layer in the acrylic “sandwich” there was glare and visible dust trapped between the sheets.

Over Easter I redesigned it, this time introducing metal. The knurled bolts and sheet metal are both 304 stainless steel, so they match closely and give a very polished result. The glare from above and the trapped dust were gone, the proportions and weight felt solid and genuinely valuable, and the chrome switch tied it together. This sketch model became one of my favourite things to come out of the project — though at this stage the cost of stainless steel was too high to justify making more.

## Influences

The raw, industrial character of the sketch model — sheet metal, exposed nuts and bolts, layered acrylic — places it close to several existing designs. MLK Furniture, a French studio working in laser-cut sheet metal, was a clear point of resemblance, though the only element I consciously borrowed was the use of bolts. The model also echoes work by Teenage Engineering and Steljes. Teenage Engineering is an active influence on my choices — geometric shapes, simple interfaces, and material honesty — and itself draws on Dieter Rams, another designer I admire.

## The wooden lamp

The first production set was made in wood: laser ply, larch, and ash. A full-size model in laser ply, built at my mother's request, had a warmer, homelier feel — best demonstrated by the plant that ended up sitting on top of it. From there the final lamps were scaled to 110mm square, matching the original sketch model and keeping material cost low enough to leave room for labour and retail margin.

I started with 7mm larch left over from a previous project. After testing, the 6mm bolt holes proved too loose and were tightened to 5.7mm to stop the layers shifting. Cutting dashed lines around the border of each acrylic square refracted the light within the sheet and cut glare better than expected — a detail that stayed for every acrylic layer afterwards. The larch was drum- then orbital-sanded and finished with oil, which also sealed the burnt laser-cut edges so they wouldn't stain when touched; the stripey edge left by the laser was kept on purpose.

The electronics were soldered at home with a soldering iron and helping hands, using lead-free solder — each lamp a USB lead, a prewired switch, and a prewired LED module. It was my first time soldering, and it turned out straightforward enough that I'd happily do it again. The finished light is a touch brighter than ideal but a good colour, with a satisfying switch on the front so there's no reaching for a cable.

## The metal lamp

The stainless-steel design had always felt the strongest, and a coursemate's leftover sheet made it viable: enough to make four steel lamps in time for Bovey Tracey. To check the numbers I built my first proper Excel costing sheet — an interactive calculator with a lookup table that returned a cost and retail price for any combination of nuts, bolts, and materials. It confirmed that the steel lamp could be cost-effective even with its pricier bolts, which are beautiful enough to be worth the difference.

This time the steel was laser-cut, since making several at once made the setup time worthwhile. Metal laser cutting leaves the parts attached by small tabs, so there was extra labour in cleaning up each cutout — worth it for a product that feels premium enough to sell for more. A consistent, professional finish came from sand blasting and scoring with sandpaper and a steel wire brush.

## The Corian lamp

Corian is a synthetic stone — a polymer that is cold to the touch, surprisingly translucent, and as heavy as stone while being far easier to sand and CNC mill. The piece I used was a jade-coloured offcut already in the workshop, and therefore free; bought new, the material cost would have been very high. That jade colouring gave the lamp a quiet maturity, with an almost medical aesthetic. High-profile examples of Corian in object design are rare, but Markus Johansson's “Ciratta Octopus Lamp” hints at the material's creative possibilities.

## Batch production

Making ten of something rewards planning. Careful packing on the laser cutter turned each 600 × 600mm sheet into two usable panels; spread across four panels, the cuts yielded eleven lamps, with the leftovers feeding a different shape. Hiding and revealing layers within a single Illustrator file made it quick to move between cutting jobs. Components were batched too — all the acrylic cut at once, all the wood prepared at once, and every lamp's electronics soldered in one sitting — which cut the time lost switching between tasks. Drum-sanding the wood before cutting let me finish six pieces in one pass.

## Deliverables

The brief asked for more than the objects themselves: a mock Bovey Tracey stand set up as a group, an online presence, the costing spreadsheet, and a press release. The spreadsheet grew into an automated calculator that adds up a lamp's cost from text inputs and factors in labour. The press release — a format I only learned of two days before the deadline — became a formal document setting out each lamp's intended context, materials, cost, dimensions, and character. For the social side I made three posters: two drawing on the minimalism of IKEA's advertising, and one early-2000s “Y2K” experiment under the fictional brand TREMAYAH, a username I use online.

## Final thoughts

I'm very happy with how this turned out. It took many hours — not only making, but designing and logging as well — and the dual pressure of selling the work while having to repeat it ten times pushed the quality up while keeping my ambitions in check. Time management remains my main weakness; I aimed to finish a week early and didn't, but I'm glad I set the goal all the same.
