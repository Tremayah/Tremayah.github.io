The brief had two parts. The first: build a *Taxonomy of Materiality* — a personal classification system documenting critical exploration of at least ten materials and ten production processes, presented as an elegantly designed PDF. The second: design a simple object optimised for two-part moulding, with CAD models, drawings, visualisations, and two physical prototypes.

## Starting point

The direction was clear early: keyboard keycaps. There is a whole community of people who customise their keyboards obsessively — but beautiful keycaps carry a significant price premium. The question was whether they could be made at home, in interesting materials, without sacrificing quality.

Materials shortlisted for investigation: brass, copper, steel, ceramic (glossy and matte), Jesmonite, wood, and glass.

## Taxonomy

Figma was chosen for the taxonomy after evaluating Freeform and Illustrator — its interface was more intuitive for a classification system of this complexity. Metals were grouped into subcategories: Everyday, Lightweight, Strong, Decorative, Precious, and Special. The final document was rebuilt in Adobe InDesign for submission — considerably more time-consuming, but significantly better to look at.

## Prototyping

**Prototype 1 — Jesmonite + Brass Powder:** The mould captured extraordinary surface detail. The pouring hole was oversized, requiring heavy post-processing. Colour came out darker than expected.

**Prototype 2 — Plain Jesmonite:** Smooth, creamy appearance. Extremely brittle — crumbled on contact. Unsuitable for actual use.

**Prototype 3 — Jesmonite + Sand:** Attempted to achieve a matte, stone-like texture. The aggregate weakened thin walls and the result was fragile.

## CAD

All 3D work was built in a single Rhino file. The design challenge was personalising a standardised keycap form while preserving the functional underside geometry required by mechanical switches. Iterations included:

- **Grippy Keycap** — surface overhangs to improve material bonding
- **Clicker Box** — housing for a mechanical switch; became an unexpectedly satisfying fidget object
- **Geometric Ergonomic Keycap** — rounded geometry, showing resin printing lines clearly
- **Mould Maker V1 and V2** — progressive refinements for plaster mould creation, scaled to 1.1× to account for shrinkage

## Ceramic process

The project pivoted to slip-cast porcelain. Plaster moulds were made with four pouring slots. Each cycle:

1. Pour liquid slip into mould
2. Wait approximately three minutes
3. Pour excess slip back out
4. Remove thin-walled greenware carefully (1–2mm walls, vulnerable to deformation)
5. Dry in hot cupboard
6. First kiln firing
7. Apply glaze
8. Second firing

Wall thickness varied with pouring duration. The thinnest walls that still left sufficient interior clearance for the switch were preferred.

## Outcome

The finished keycaps are glazed porcelain with concealed Jesmonite components. They feel good to touch. They came out smaller than expected — shrinkage from the second firing combined with the trapezoidal proportions of a keycap makes the final piece look compact. They are mounted and working on a Bluetooth numpad.
